## H1
Data Dashboards for SMEs: What to Track and Why

## Introduction (150 words)

Everyone agrees that data-driven decisions are better than gut feel. But when you look at many SME dashboards, you see the same problem: walls of numbers that nobody looks at.

The issue isn't technology. It's clarity. Most dashboards fail because they track everything instead of what matters.

This guide helps you think clearly about business dashboards: what makes them useful, which metrics deserve space on your screen, and how to avoid the common traps that turn dashboards into digital wallpaper.

**What we'll cover:**
- Why most dashboards fail (and how to avoid it)
- The hierarchy of metrics: what to track at each level
- Metrics by function: operations, finance, sales, marketing
- Dashboard design principles that work
- Build vs. buy: choosing the right approach
- Getting started: practical next steps

---

## H2: Why Most Dashboards Fail

Let's start with the hard truth: most dashboards get ignored.

Why? The same three reasons, over and over.

### Problem 1: Too Many Metrics

The dashboard shows 47 KPIs. The user's eyes glaze over. Nothing stands out because everything is competing for attention.

**The fix:** Ruthless prioritisation. If a metric doesn't drive a decision, it doesn't deserve dashboard space.

### Problem 2: No Actionability

The dashboard shows that "website traffic is up 12%"—but what should you do about it? Many metrics are interesting but not actionable.

**The fix:** Every metric should connect to a potential action. "Traffic is up" becomes "traffic from paid campaigns is down while organic is up—reallocate budget?"

### Problem 3: Stale or Untrustworthy Data

The dashboard updates weekly, but decisions happen daily. Or worse: people don't trust the numbers because they've seen errors before.

**The fix:** Update frequency that matches decision cycles. And rigorous data quality—because a wrong number is worse than no number.

## H2: The Hierarchy of Metrics

Different people need different views. A dashboard that works for the MD won't work for an operations manager.

### Level 1: Executive Dashboard (MD/CEO)

**Purpose:** High-level health check. "Is the business on track?"

**Key Metrics:**
- Revenue vs. target
- Cash position
- Key operational metric (varies by business)
- Customer/client trend (acquisition, churn)
- One leading indicator specific to your model

**Update Frequency:** Daily or weekly

**Design:** Single screen. Traffic light indicators. Drill-down available but not required.

---

### Level 2: Functional Dashboard (Department Heads)

**Purpose:** Monitor and manage the function. "What needs my attention?"

**Key Metrics:** Function-specific (see next section)

**Update Frequency:** Daily, sometimes real-time

**Design:** More detail than executive view. Filter capability. Exception highlighting.

---

### Level 3: Operational Dashboard (Team Members)

**Purpose:** Execute the work. "What do I need to do today?"

**Key Metrics:** Task-level metrics and queues

**Update Frequency:** Real-time or near-real-time

**Design:** Focused on workflow. Clear priorities. Minimal distraction.

## H2: Metrics by Function

What should you track? Here's a starting point for common functions.

### Operations Metrics

**Primary:**
- Order/job completion rate
- On-time delivery/SLA compliance
- Utilisation (people, equipment, space)
- Quality/defect rate

**Secondary:**
- Backlog size and age
- Capacity vs. demand
- Exception/escalation count
- Process cycle time

**Red Flags to Surface:**
- SLA breach incoming
- Capacity constraint emerging
- Quality trend deteriorating

---

### Finance Metrics

**Primary:**
- Cash position and runway
- Revenue (actual vs. budget)
- Gross margin
- Aged debtors summary

**Secondary:**
- Cost trends by category
- Working capital ratios
- Revenue by segment/product
- Forecast vs. actual variance

**Red Flags to Surface:**
- Cash flow warning
- Margin compression
- Overdue invoices aging

---

### Sales Metrics

**Primary:**
- Pipeline value by stage
- Win rate trend
- Revenue closed (vs. target)
- Average deal size

**Secondary:**
- Activity metrics (calls, meetings)
- Time in stage (deals stuck)
- Lead-to-customer conversion
- Sales by rep/region

**Red Flags to Surface:**
- Pipeline insufficient for target
- Win rate declining
- Deals stuck in stage

---

### Marketing Metrics

**Primary:**
- Lead volume (qualified)
- Cost per lead
- Conversion rate by channel
- ROI by campaign

**Secondary:**
- Traffic trends
- Email engagement
- Brand search volume
- Attribution data

**Red Flags to Surface:**
- Lead quality declining
- CAC rising
- Channel underperforming

## H2: Dashboard Design Principles

Good dashboards share common design principles. Here are the ones that matter most.

### Principle 1: One Question Per View

Each dashboard screen should answer one clear question. "How is sales performing?" is different from "What does our pipeline look like?" Don't combine them.

### Principle 2: Comparison Over Absolute Numbers

"Revenue is £245,000" means nothing without context. Revenue vs. target, vs. last month, vs. same month last year—that's useful.

### Principle 3: Visual Hierarchy

The most important thing should be the most visually prominent. Use size, colour, and position to guide the eye.

### Principle 4: Exceptions Demand Attention

Dashboards should surface anomalies automatically. Colour coding, conditional formatting, and alert indicators help users spot what needs attention.

### Principle 5: Drill-Down, Don't Overwhelm

Start with the summary. Make it easy to dig deeper. But don't show all the detail by default.

### Principle 6: Trust Through Transparency

Show when data was last updated. Show the source. Build confidence through clarity.

## H2: Build vs. Buy

Should you use off-the-shelf tools or build custom dashboards?

### When Off-the-Shelf Works:

- Your needs are standard for your industry
- You use common data sources with built-in connectors
- You have technical skills internally to configure and maintain
- You want speed to deployment

**Good options:** Power BI, Tableau, Looker Studio, Metabase

### When Custom Makes Sense:

- Your metrics or data sources are unusual
- You need tight integration with specific workflows
- Off-the-shelf tools couldn't be configured despite trying
- You want full control without vendor dependency

**Typical approach:** Python + Streamlit, Dash, or similar frameworks

### The Hybrid Path:

Many SMEs do well with off-the-shelf tools for standard analytics, plus custom builds for specific operational needs. There's no rule that says you must choose one.

## H2: Getting Started

Ready to improve your dashboards? Here's a practical path.

### Step 1: Audit What You Have (1-2 hours)

Look at your existing reports and dashboards. Ask:
- How often do people actually look at these?
- What decisions do they inform?
- What's missing that people ask for?

### Step 2: List Your Key Decisions (1 hour)

What decisions do leaders and managers make regularly? For each, what data would help them decide faster or better?

### Step 3: Prioritise Metrics (1 hour)

From your decision list, derive the metrics that matter. Ruthlessly cut anything that's "nice to know" but doesn't drive action.

### Step 4: Choose Your Tool (Variable)

Based on your situation (data sources, technical skills, budget), select an approach: off-the-shelf, custom, or hybrid.

### Step 5: Start with One Dashboard

Don't try to build everything at once. Pick the most valuable dashboard—often the executive view—and get it right. Learn. Then expand.

## H2: Summary

The best dashboards are the ones people actually use. That requires ruthless focus on what matters, clear design, and trustworthy data.

**Key takeaways:**
- Less is more—track what drives decisions, not everything you can
- Different levels of the organisation need different views
- Design matters: hierarchy, comparison, exception highlighting
- Build vs. buy depends on your situation; hybrid often works
- Start with one dashboard, get it right, then expand
